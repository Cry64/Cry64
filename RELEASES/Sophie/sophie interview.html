Schizoscriptures Interview

Schizoscriptures is the Experimental Rock / Digital Hardcore project created by Arizona musician Sophie. Previously known as "Queen Sophie's VCR and the Bright Side of Hemisphere 5", Sophie currently has six projects on her bandcamp, the latest of which released under the moniker "Schizoscriptures."

"First off, what is the origin behind the name "schizoscriptures", it's cool as fuck"

"Thank you, that's part of why I chose it. (It sounds cool) My best friend does a lot of conlang stuff, like designing languages, and we had both spoken about weird experimental scripts for music, with weird sort of schizophrenic/apparition symbolism. Then later I was listening to death grips, the song Hunger Games, and Mc Ride in one of the lines says "Schizo Superscript Voice imprint only I can hear you" and I was like hm.... I had also made a song called subscript as one of my first songs in a DAW a really long time ago, and I just decided I had to use "script" in the name like that. Also it's easier to remember than Queen Sophie's VCR & The Bright Side of Hemisphere 5".

"You mentioned your first d.a.w, could you explain how you first started creating music"?

"This is a ridiculous paragraph but hear me out; around 6th grade or so they rolled out chromebooks in schools, which if you somehow don't know, are like these terrible pseudo-laptops that can only run a browser and some really simple programs. Anyway my friends and I used to like, basically LARP fantasy scenarios every single day for hours at a time at recess which is kind of wild but it was absolutely fantastic, I don't regret any of it. We would spend hours drawing characters and writing about lore and designing all this crazy stuff. We were like, 13 or something, so we were SO inspired by undertale and earthbound and like, just RPG games with crazy wacky characters and cool music. I Found a website that I could use on the chromebooks at that time, Beepbox.co, which is like this sort of 8-bit tracker thing, and inspired by game soundtracks, I started just making hot garbage in it, every single day, for hours at a time, for our little fantasy thing. It got way better with time and I eventually moved to using FL studio and pursuing making music for myself / more personal reasons, but I still really love game development and fantasy story stuff. If you want to use the beepbox that I actually used you have to search for "beepbox 2.3" because they've added so much now it's like a full daw, it has FM synthesis and stuff. I love that site so much."

"It's safe to say that your music spans atleast 10 different genres, what experiences and influences do you think have shaped your sound?"

"For the genres; I think maybe I come from a weird place, for music. I never even listened to any music for most of my life until more recent years, when I really really got into making music. I actually started making music BEFORE I had begun really listening to other people's music much. I never really got the chance to physically play with other people or improvise live until very recently but I really love live sounding music. All music I've experienced has been a very MODERN THING I think, where its a finalized product, 20hz-20khz with two channels and THAT'S IT. Like a product, or a file. Music isn't some like, action, or a cultural thing, it's like a painting or end product to me, a lot. All you get is sounds and you have to just paint stuff together. I don't know much about classical approaches to music, or even physical technique, so I sort of absorb all music as these textures, and colors and visual landscapes in my mind. When I make music it's just smashing textures I like together until it feels right. Basically I am not paying attention to genres as much, I'm just slamming songs and ideas I liked into a texture as much as possible. Experiences though... that's a different question because uh… what the music is about, and what experiences im putting into it is a lot of different/varied stuff I think."

"Speaking of live music, I noticed on your Instagram that you now have a drummer! What does this mean for the future of your music, and what can we expect in your next project?"

"I met the drummer at a 100 gecs show when they forced me into their group despite me being extremely antisocial. They saw my death grips shirt. It was very nice of them, I had a lot of fun. It was a good concert. I envy them a little bit, because im making all my music with like, a keyboard and a mouse on a monitor with headphones alone in a dark room, visually, in a sterile way, and they get to just jam the fuck out on a LOUD and fun instrument, and really FEEL the music, literally, with huge speakers and stuff. I'm hoping that playing live, incorporating live drumming, and working with a live drummer in general will sort of infuse that energy into the music, where it's less manufactured and sterile, and more of us going absolutely bonkers and really leaning into repetitive, strenuous or even painfully violent performances. It can be more visceral. I've been really inspired by harsh walls of noise, and warm textures recently, like Sunn o))) and Prolaps. The Drummer loves black metal and has done a lot of punk drumming. They're really into the digital hardcore thing. I really like what they're doing. It'll be good and very angry, violent I think."

"What do you think of the current underground landscape, do you have any other artists in mind that stand out to you?"

"I'm not really sure what counts as underground sorry hehe,,,, I get most music... no... all my music from the internet so it cant be THAT underground. I live in Arizona and the music scene is rather dry I think, or maybe I'm just not invited to any insane underground shows."

"lastly, any words for those who listen to your music, or want to make music"?

"if you want to make music.... immediately attempt to make whatever you want to make. You legitimately become good at it by just trying it over and over (its magic). Also, art isn't about how hard it is to make, or how long it takes, or who is watching, or anything like that, it's about how it makes you feel individually, that's the valuable part. Even if you don't feel like it, It'll work out later. So don't feel bad about doing ANYTHING you feel like, even if it’s not financially successful or none of your friends get it or something. (my family hates my music). Thanks >:)"
